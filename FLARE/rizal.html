Republic Act No. 1425 - The Rizal Law

Historical Background

Passed: June 12, 1956

Author: Senator Claro M. Recto
Sponsor: Senator José P. Laurel

Republic Act No. 1425, commonly known as the Rizal Law, was enacted to include the life, works, and writings of Dr. José Rizal, particularly his novels Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, in the curricula of all schools, colleges, and universities in the Philippines.

Purpose and Objectives

  • Foster nationalism, patriotism, and civic duty among Filipinos, especially the youth
  • Combat ignorance of Filipino history and identity
  • Resist foreign ideological domination
  • Promote national consciousness and moral integrity

Key Provisions

  • Compulsory reading of Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo in unexpurgated form
  • Rizal's life and works must be part of school curricula
  • Books must be available in school libraries and Filipino translations
  • Religious exemption clause for conscientious objectors

Controversies and Opposition

The bill sparked intense debate, particularly from the Catholic Church, who viewed Rizal's novels as anti-clerical and blasphemous. Catholic groups protested, claiming it would undermine religious faith. Despite opposition, Senator Laurel negotiated a compromise allowing religious exemptions, and the bill became law on Philippine Independence Day.

The World of 19th Century Philippines

The 19th century during Spanish colonization was a dynamic and creative age. New ideas about freedom, nationalism, and reform were spreading, while the Philippines underwent significant changes in economy, society, politics, and education.

Economic Development

Encomienda to Hacienda System

Encomienda: Spanish settlers controlled native Filipinos in exchange for protection and Christianization, often abused by encomenderos.

Hacienda: Large agricultural estates owned by Spanish families or religious orders where natives worked, often exploited.

Galleon Trade

Trade route between Manila and Acapulco, Mexico (1565–1815). Brought wealth to some but limited local industries.

Forced Labor Systems

Polo y Servicio: Filipino males (16–60) required to work 40 days yearly on public projects, often unpaid.

Bandala: Provinces forced to sell products to government at low prices.

Social Hierarchy

Peninsulares - Spaniards born in Spain; held highest positions in government and Church
Insulares - Spaniards born in Philippines (Creoles); second in social rank
Mestizos - Mixed ancestry: Spanish Mestizos (Spanish and native), Chinese Mestizos (Chinese and native, many became wealthy traders)
Principalia - Native Filipino nobility; wealthy and influential locals with voting rights
Ilustrados - Educated Filipinos (like Rizal) who studied in Europe and promoted reform
Tornatras - Mixed Filipino, Chinese, and Spanish ancestry
Indios - Common native Filipinos; lowest social rank, often poor and uneducated

Political Structure

Spanish Monarch - The King of Spain, ultimate ruler of the Philippines
Ministro de Ultramar - Spanish Minister for Overseas Colonies, based in Spain
Governor-General - King's representative in Philippines; highest authority
Royal Audiencia - Highest court in the colony
Provincial Government

• Alcaldia - Provinces under full Spanish control, headed by Alcalde Mayor
• Corrigimiento - Areas not fully pacified, led by Corregidor (military men)

Municipal Government

• Gobernadorcillo - Town mayor (often from principalia)
• Teniente Mayor - Assistant to gobernadorcillo
• Teniente de Policia - Peace and order
• Teniente Sementeras - Agricultural lands
• Teniente de Ganados - Livestock

City Government

• Ayuntamiento (Cabildo) - City council for important cities like Manila
• Alcalde Mayor - City governor
• Regidores - Councilors
• Aguacil Mayor - Chief of police
• Escribano - Notary or clerk

Barangay Level

• Cabeza de Barangay - Local village leader, responsible for collecting taxes
• Guardia Civil - Colonial police force, often abused power against Filipinos
• Friars - Spanish priests controlling religious, educational, and political aspects

Government Oversight Systems

Residencia

Review of a governor's performance after his term ended

Visita

Surprise inspection of government officials during their tenure

Indulto de Comercio

Trading privilege given to officials like Governor-General for personal gain

Educational System Timeline

1565
Start of Spanish colonization; education primarily religious, run by friars for converting natives
1589
Jesuits established first formal schools, including Colegio de Manila, focusing on Spanish and Latin
1601
Colegio de San Ignacio founded for elite Filipino boys to become priests
1611
University of Santo Tomas established by Dominicans - oldest existing university in Asia
1640s
Religious orders (Augustinians, Franciscans, Recollects) opened more schools
1768
Jesuits expelled from Philippines by Spanish royal order, temporarily closing their schools
1783
Real Sociedad Economica de Amigos del Pais founded, advocating vocational and practical education
1839
Opening of Colegio de San Juan de Letran to Filipino youth, including non-Spanish.
1855
Governor General Manuel Crespo pushed for the improvement of elementary education in the Philippines.
1861
A comprehensive report on the Philippine educational system was completed, identifying flaws and inequality.
1863
Educational Decree of 1863:
• Primary education became free and compulsory for children aged 6-12.
• Required schools for boys and girls in each town.
• Created Normal School for Teachers in Manila.
• Established a supervisory structure for schools under the civil government.
1871
UST opened Faculty of Medicine and Pharmacy, expanding higher education options
1880s
Rise of the Ilustrados - educated Filipinos like Rizal demanding reforms

Defects of the Educational System

Religious Emphasis

Focused more on catechism and Christian teachings than practical knowledge

Academic Absence of Freedom

No room for critical thinking or open discussion of ideas

Racial Discrimination

Preference given to Spaniards and mestizos in educational access

Limited Curriculum

Only basic subjects: reading, writing, arithmetic, and religion

Rizal's Family, Childhood and Early Education

The Rizal Family

Francisco Engracio Rizal Mercado y Alejandro

May 11, 1818 – January 5, 1898

Rizal's father; well-educated farmer from Biñan, Laguna. Studied Latin and philosophy at Colegio de San Jose. A model of discipline and industry.

Teodora Alonso Realonda y Quintos

November 9, 1827 – August 16, 1911

Rizal's mother; known for intelligence and strong character. Rizal's first teacher and highly influential in shaping his values.

The Rizal Siblings

Saturnina

1850 – 1913

Saturnina Rizal Mercado de Hidalgo

Lifespan: 1850 – 1913

Description: The eldest sister. She supported Rizal's education and managed family affairs. She married Manuel T. Hidalgo of Tanauan, Batangas.

Paciano

1851 – 1930

Paciano Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda

Lifespan: 1851 – 1930

Description: Only brother of Jose. He was a patriot and supporter of the Propaganda Movement. After Jose's death, he became a general in the Philippine Revolution.

Narcisa

1852 – 1939

Narcisa Rizal Mercado–López

Lifespan: 1852 – 1939

Description: She helped finance Rizal's studies and visited him during his exile in Dapitan. She searched for his burial place after his execution.

Olympia

1855 – 1887

Olympia Rizal Mercado-Ubaldo

Lifespan: 1855 – 1887

Description: Close to Jose; she was the mother of Rizal's first nephew. She died young due to childbirth.

Lucia

1857 – 1919

Lucia Rizal Mercado–Herbosa

Lifespan: 1857 – 1919

Description: Married Mariano Herbosa, who died of cholera and was denied a Christian burial because of his relation to Rizal. She supported Jose in his endeavors.

Maria

1859 – 1945

Maria Rizal Mercado

Lifespan: 1859 – 1945

Description: Known as kind and supportive. She took care of Rizal's belongings after his death. She had a daughter named Encarnacion who kept many of Rizal's letters.

José

1861 – 1896

Jose Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonzo Realonda

Lifespan: 1861 – 1896

Description: Philippine national hero. A doctor, writer, and reformist. He used his writings to expose the abuses of the Spanish colonial government and was executed for his beliefs.

Concepcion

1862 – 1865

Concepcion "Concha" Rizal

Lifespan: 1862 – 1865

Description: Died at age 3. Her death deeply affected young Jose; she was his closest sibling during his childhood.

Josefa

1865 – 1945

Josefa Rizal Mercado

Lifespan: 1865 – 1945

Description: A member of the Katipunan, unmarried and epileptic. She was brave and devoted to the cause of the Filipino revolution.

Trinidad

1868 – 1951

Trinidad Rizal Mercado

Lifespan: 1868 – 1951

Description: She was with Jose during his final hours and kept the alcohol stove where he hid his last poem, Mi Último Adiós. She never married and lived a long life preserving her brother's memory.

Soledad

1870 – 1929

Soledad Rizal Mercado-Quintero

Lifespan: 1870 – 1929

Description: The youngest sibling. She became a teacher and was the only one to marry someone younger than her (Pantaleon Quintero). Rizal had high hopes for her.

Childhood Years in Calamba

A Nurturing Home

Rizal grew up in a peaceful and prosperous environment in Calamba, surrounded by a loving family that valued education and moral character.

His Mother, His First Teacher

Doña Teodora Alonso was his first and most influential teacher. She taught him the alphabet and prayers at age 3, and by age 5, he could read and write fluently.

Early Signs of Brilliance

Young Rizal showed exceptional abilities in storytelling, artistic talents (drawing, painting, sculpting), and intellectual curiosity that amazed his family.

The Story of the Moth

His mother's story about a moth drawn to flame deeply impacted his views on sacrifice and seeking knowledge, foreshadowing his own martyrdom.

Family Devotion

The family gathered for daily Angelus prayers. He had a special bond with sister Concha, whose death at age 3 was his first great sorrow.

Love for Learning

He read the Spanish Family Bible, frequently attended church, and learned about sports, Latin, and nature from his uncles.

Early Education

Age 3-5

First Teacher: Teodora Alonso

Learned alphabet, prayers, reading, and writing from his mother. By age 5, he could read and write fluently.

Age 6-8

Private Tutors

Maestro Celestino - First private tutor
Maestro Lucas Padua - Taught science
Leon Monroy - Spanish and Latin instruction (died after 5 months)

Age 9

Biñan School

Studied under Maestro Justiniano Aquino Cruz, excelled in Latin and Spanish

Age 11-16

Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872-1877)

Graduated with highest honors (Bachelor of Arts), excelled in all subjects, especially Latin, Spanish, science, and humanities

Age 16-21

University of Santo Tomas (1877-1882)

Studied Philosophy and Letters, then Medicine (ophthalmology). Experienced discrimination from Spanish friars.

First Loves and Family Pain

Segunda Katigbak

Fell in love at age 16 with Segunda from Lipa, Batangas, who was already engaged to another.

Leonor Valenzuela

Wrote secret love letters using invisible ink made from salt water that became visible when heated.

Leonor Rivera

Met his long-time love who would later inspire the character of Maria Clara in his novels.

Mother's Imprisonment

His mother Teodora Alonso was unjustly imprisoned in 1872 on false charges of poisoning, released after two and a half years.

Life Abroad and the Making of a Hero

Higher Education and European Studies

Secret Departure to Spain (1882)

Left the Philippines secretly in 1882 to study in Europe, fearing Spanish authorities. His family was unaware of his departure until he was already abroad.

Madrid, Spain (1882-1885)

University: Universidad Central de Madrid (now Complutense University)

Achievements: Earned Licentiate in Medicine (1884), completed Philosophy and Letters (1885)

Activities: Joined the Propaganda Movement, contributed to La Solidaridad, became a Freemason advocating liberal reforms

Paris, France (1885-1886)

Focus: Advanced studies in ophthalmology under famous Dr. Louis de Wecker

Purpose: To specialize in eye surgery for his mother's deteriorating eyesight

Heidelberg, Germany (1886)

Institution: University of Heidelberg

Achievements: Practical training in ophthalmology under Dr. Otto Becker, wrote "A Las Flores del Heidelberg", became fluent in German

Literary Masterpieces

Noli Me Tangere

Published: 1887 in Berlin, Germany

Content: Exposed the abuses of Spanish friars and officials in the Philippines

Impact: Stirred both admiration and outrage; banned in the Philippines by Spanish authorities

Aftermath: Received death threats from Spanish friars, branded as traitor and anti-Catholic

El Filibusterismo

Published: 1891 in Ghent, Belgium

Theme: Darker and more revolutionary than Noli Me Tangere

Purpose: To inspire change through realization of injustice and need for reform

Travels and Cultural Observations

Italy, Austria, Switzerland

Cultural exposure and intellectual exchange with European scholars and artists, studying different forms of government and society.

United Kingdom

Studied English and researched historical works about the Philippines in the British Museum library.

Belgium and Netherlands

Wrote and published El Filibusterismo. Observed different colonial approaches and governance systems.

Japan

Observed Japanese discipline and cleanliness; brief romance with Seiko Usui (O-Sei-San). Admired their progress.

United States

Noted racial discrimination and social issues; compared American colonial attitudes with Philippine situation.

Hong Kong and Macau

Worked as an ophthalmologist and planned peaceful reforms for the Philippines. Strategic location for communications.

Singapore and Asia

Analyzed different colonial systems and compared them to the Philippine experience under Spanish rule.

O-Sei-San (Seiko Usui)

From: Japan

Story: "Gettie" fell in love with Rizal while he stayed with her family. He left to avoid distraction from his goals

From: France
Story: Of Spanish and Filipino descent. Almost engaged but ended due to religious differences and fellow reformists' interference

Suzanne Jacoby

From: Brussels, Belgium
Story: Rizal lodged with her family. She admired him deeply but he chose not to pursue the relationship

Josephine Bracken

From: Hong Kong
Story: Came to Dapitan to seek treatment for stepfather. Lived with Rizal, his "dulce extranjera." Witnessed his execution

Leonor Rivera - The Great Love

From: Camiling, Tarlac (1867-1893)
The Story: Rizal's most serious relationship lasting 11 years (1880-1891). She inspired the character Maria Clara in Noli Me Tangere. Her mother disapproved and hid Rizal's letters. She later married Henry Kipping, a British engineer, which deeply broke Rizal's heart. She died young, possibly from illness and heartbreak.

Return to the Philippines and Martyrdom

Return and Exile (1892)

Returned to Philippines in 1892 and founded La Liga Filipina, a civic organization seeking peaceful reform. Was immediately arrested and exiled to Dapitan, Zamboanga del Norte (1892-1896) where he:

  • Practiced medicine and treated patients
  • Taught school to local children
  • Improved the town's water system and infrastructure
  • Conducted scientific research and collected specimens
  • Lived peacefully with Josephine Bracken

Final Journey and Arrest (1896)

Volunteered as a doctor to serve in Cuba during an epidemic, but was arrested en route in 1896. Charged with rebellion due to his writings and alleged connections to revolutionaries, despite advocating peaceful reform.

Execution and Eternal Legacy

Imprisoned in Fort Santiago, Manila. On December 30, 1896, at dawn, he was executed by firing squad at Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park). His martyrdom sparked the Philippine Revolution and cemented his legacy as the National Hero. His final poem "Mi Último Adiós" became a testament to his love for country.
